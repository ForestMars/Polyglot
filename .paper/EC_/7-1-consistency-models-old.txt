The consistency model hierarchy has been extensively studied, from weak consistency through causal consistency to sequential consistency and linearizability [69, 72]. Vogels [37] provided an influential taxonomy of eventual consistency variants for large-scale systems. Bailis et al. [66] characterized the practical tradeoffs between consistency and availability across this hierarchy. Viotti and Vukolic [67] provided a comprehensive formal treatment of consistency models for replicated data, establishing the definitional framework our work extends.

Eventual coherence does not occupy a new position on this hierarchy. It challenges the hierarchy’s foundational assumption: that the correct objective for a replicated system is convergence to identical state. The hierarchy asks how strongly replicas agree. Eventual coherence asks a different question: is each replica’s state valid relative to its own history? We prove in Proposition 1 that these questions are not comparable -- eventual coherence neither implies nor is implied by eventual consistency. This is not a weaker guarantee of the same property. It is a different property serving a different correctness objective.

Ahamad et al. [39] formalized causal consistency as requiring that causally related operations be observed in the same order across all replicas. Eventual coherence provides this property within each device’s synchronization epoch but explicitly permits divergence across epochs when causal histories are incompatible. We ground causal ordering in the happens-before relation via Lamport clocks [13] rather than physical timestamps, avoiding the well-known pitfalls of physical clock-based causal reasoning that Lamport identified in his original work [13].
